📚 Contract Types Explained

Understanding contract vehicles is critical for positioning. Each type serves different purposes and requires different approaches.

Type Full Name Purpose Typical Value Best For
RFI Request for Information Market research, shapes future RFPs. No contract yet. N/A Early positioning, requirement shaping
BAA Broad Agency Announcement R&D funding in loosely defined areas. White paper first. $100K - $50M Breakthrough concepts, startups
OTA Other Transaction Authority Fast prototyping without FAR compliance. Startup-friendly. $500K - $100M Rapid hardware demos
CSO Commercial Solutions Opening DIU's OTA variant. Near-commercial tech acquisition. $1M - $20M VC-backed companies
FAR RFP Federal Acquisition Regulation RFP Traditional full procurement. Heavy compliance required. $10M - $1B+ Production programs, primes
IDIQ Indefinite Delivery/Quantity Pre-qualified vendor pool, task orders issued later. Varies by order Ongoing services, established vendors
SBIR/STTR Small Business Innovation Research Small business R&D funding, 3-phase ladder to production. $150K - $2M Startups, small businesses
CRADA Cooperative R&D Agreement Partner with national labs on R&D. Shared IP terms. Varies Lab access, testing

🔔 How to Set Up Contract Alerts

Follow this checklist to ensure you never miss an opportunity. Smart companies treat RFIs like mini-proposals.

  • 1. Register on SAM.gov

    Create account at sam.gov. Get your UEI (Unique Entity ID) and CAGE code. Required for all federal contracts. This is mandatory before you can bid on anything.

  • 2. Set Up SAM.gov Saved Searches

    Keywords: "space nuclear", "fission surface power", "nuclear thermal", "reactor", "HALEU", "cislunar power", "space power", "energy conversion". Enable email notifications for all saved searches.

  • 3. Register on SBIR.gov

    Create account at sbir.gov. Subscribe to topic areas: nuclear, space technology, power systems, thermal management, advanced materials.

  • 4. Monitor DARPA Weekly (No Auto-Alerts)

    DARPA doesn't have automated alerts - manually check DARPA opportunities every Monday. Watch for TTO and DSO BAAs.

  • 5. Follow NASA FSP Program

    Bookmark NASA Glenn FSP page. Industry days are announced before SAM.gov postings. Attend all industry days and webinars.

  • 6. Register for DSIP (DoD SBIR/STTR)

    Defense SBIR/STTR Innovation Portal at dodsbirsttr.mil for DoD proposal submission system access.

  • 7. Watch DOE BAA Schedule

    DOE releases BAAs first Wednesday monthly. Check energy.gov and SAM.gov. Keywords: DOE nuclear space power, reactor, HALEU.

  • 8. Register on NASA NSPIRES

    Create account at nspires.nasaprs.com for NASA research opportunities and NOIs (Notices of Intent).

🌑 NASA - Fission Surface Power Program

NASA's primary vehicle for lunar nuclear power. FSP is a formal technology-development program led by NASA in partnership with DOE. Think of FSP as the umbrella effort under which studies, contracts, lab work, and industry competitions all live.

2030 Goal: Lunar Surface Reactor

NASA declared intent to put a nuclear reactor on the Moon by Q1 FY2030. Recently signed MOU with DOE solidifies collaboration and advances deployment of nuclear reactors on the Moon and in orbit. The system will provide at least 40 kWe - enough to continuously run 30 households for ten years.

Program Overview

  • Target: 40-100+ kWe systems for lunar surface
  • Timeline: Q1 FY2030 deployment goal
  • Partnership: NASA + DOE + Industry
  • Phase 1 concept contracts: ~$5M each (2022-2025)
  • Must operate for years without refueling
  • Survive 14-day lunar nights
  • Support: long-duration missions, habitats, ISRU, science ops, cislunar infrastructure

Current Awardees

  • Westinghouse - eVinci-derived microreactor concept (with Astrobotic)

  • Multiple ~$5M DEEP phase contracts awarded

♕ Contract Strategy & BD Playbook

📈 The 5-Phase BD Roadmap

Space micro-reactors won't jump straight to billion-dollar buys. They move in a staircase: Market shaping → OTA prototypes → Ground demos → Flight test → FAR production.

0
Phase 0: Market Creation & Mission Shaping
Before programs exist. Create demand, define missions, influence requirements, unlock budgets.

Activities: White papers, lunar-night analyses, industry conferences, prime briefings, Hill engagement

Vehicles: Study contracts, SBIR Phase I, Concept BAAs

Output: "Nuclear required" narrative, draft requirements, agency champions

1
Phase 1: Concept & Risk Reduction
First technical money. Show space-adaptable reactor designs work on paper and in labs.

Activities: Space-variant core designs, thermal-vacuum modeling, autonomy concepts, launch safety strawmen

Vehicles: OTA prototypes, SBIR Phase II, BAAs

Output: Credible space reactor architecture, invitations to bigger demos

2
Phase 2: Integrated Ground Prototype
Full nuclear system on Earth. Run complete power system in test facilities.

Activities: Reactor hardware, control systems, power modules, safety packages, prime teaming

Vehicles: Large OTAs, Prototype BAAs ($5-25M)

Output: Government-validated reactor, "Reactor OEM" status

3
Phase 3: Flight Demonstration Mission
First real deployment in space. Launch and operate reactor in orbit or on surface.

Activities: Flight core delivery, fuel coordination, nuclear safety inputs, ops modeling

Vehicles: Hybrid OTA to FAR demo ($20-100M+)

Output: Flight heritage, default supplier positioning

4
Phase 4: Operational Procurement
Infrastructure, not experiments. Buy fleets and sustain them for decades. Multi-billion dollar programs.

Activities: Scale factories, secure HALEU supply, classified facilities, lifecycle ops

Vehicles: FAR-based production contracts, IDIQ vehicles

Output: Multi-billion-dollar programs, strategic-asset status

⚙ Technical Overview: Space Nuclear Reactors

🌍 Why Space Reactors Are Different

Space reactors must operate in brutally hostile conditions that Earth-based systems never face.

Environmental Challenges

  • Vacuum - No convective cooling possible
  • Microgravity - Affects fluid dynamics
  • Temperature swings - Lunar day/night: +260F to -280F
  • Radiation & micrometeoroids - Constant bombardment
  • Abrasive dust - Lunar regolith is sharp and clingy

Design Implications

  • Heat rejection relies on radiators only
  • Materials must survive massive thermal cycling
  • Moving parts minimized - passive systems dominate
  • Reliability beats efficiency in space
  • Mass is existential - every kg costs ~$5K-50K to orbit

🔥 How Fission Power Works

Fission is achieved by bombarding uranium atoms with neutrons. The uranium absorbs the neutron, becomes unstable, and splits apart - releasing energy plus more neutrons that continue the chain reaction.

Power Control

The amount of power is set by the number of neutrons in flight. Control this by:

  • Boron blades - Absorb neutrons when inserted ("boron poison")
  • Graphite reflectors - Bounce neutrons back into core
  • Slide boron in front of graphite to capture neutrons and reduce power

💫 Cooling & Power Conversion

Cooling Systems

  • Liquid-metal coolants - Sodium, NaK, lithium
  • Heat pipes - Embedded in core (passive)
  • Helium gas - Radiant's approach (can't become radioactive)

Power Conversion

  • Stirling engines - Good efficiency, moving parts
  • Brayton turbines - High power density
  • Thermoelectrics - Lower efficiency but ultra-reliable

☢ Fuel Technologies

HALEU

High-Assay Low-Enriched Uranium (5-20% U-235). Higher enrichment than commercial reactors but still below weapons-grade. Enables compact cores.

TRISO

Tri-structural isotropic particles. Tiny fuel kernels with multiple protective ceramic layers. Will not melt down - inherently safe.

Zirconium Hydride

Radiant's moderator innovation. Lets you pack fission physics into shipping-container-scale systems.

Helium Coolant

Cannot become radioactive in the reactor. Simplifies safety case and reduces activation concerns.

🤖 Autonomy Requirements

Space reactors operate with no operators, no spare parts, no quick repairs. They must:

  • Self-start and self-regulate
  • Detect faults autonomously
  • Scram (emergency shutdown) without human intervention
  • Reconfigure loads automatically
  • Run for a decade+ untouched

Think: Nuclear plant + spacecraft flight computer in one box.

🚀 Launch & Deployment

  • Reactor must be sub-critical during launch
  • Survive vibration and abort scenarios
  • Only activate after safe deployment
  • Pass Presidential-level nuclear approval
  • Comply with UN treaties and international agreements
⚠

Launch approval is the long pole in the tent. Start this process early - it can take years.

📚 Historical Context: Space Nuclear Programs

🇺🇸 SNAP-10A (USA, 1965)

The only U.S. fission reactor flown in space.

Mission Details

  • Purpose: Prove fission-electric power in orbit
  • Power: ~600 watts electric
  • Duration: 43 days of operation
  • Status: Still in orbit today

Outcome

  • Started successfully - proved the concept
  • Shut down due to spacecraft electrical failure (not reactor)
  • Verdict: Technical success, duration failure

🇷🇺 Soviet/Russian Space Reactors (1967-1988)

The real "workhorse" history - over 30 fission reactors flown.

BES-5 "Buk" Reactors (RORSAT Program)

  • Purpose: Power ocean surveillance radar satellites
  • Count: 32 satellites launched with nuclear sources
  • Lifetimes: 44-131 days operational

🔄 The Real Workflow: How Programs Actually Happen

Programs start with "market research," not contracts. Before money flows, agencies publish signals. Here's the actual sequence:

Step 1

Problem Discovery

BD spends time with operators, program offices, labs, integrators, HQ staff, and congressional staffers. Learning: where power is limiting missions, what's in future plans, what's unfunded but desired, which offices control money.

Step 2

Opportunity Shaping (Pre-Solicitation)

Before anything is released: white papers, technical concept notes, informal briefings, industry days, demonstrations, site visits, workshops. Goal: influence how the government frames the problem. This is called "capture shaping."

Step 3

Pick the Right Contract Path

BD helps steer toward: BAA (open R&D calls), SBIR/STTR (small business), OTA (fast prototyping), or traditional RFP (major procurements).

Step 4

Build Teams

Rarely do startups go alone. BD assembles prime contractors, integrators, national labs, universities, launch providers, test sites. Negotiates teaming agreements, workshare, IP, and margins.

Step 5

Track Budgets & Policy

BD watches: Program Objective Memorandum (POM - 5-year DoD budget), authorization bills, appropriations, Space Force/NASA strategy docs, DOE nuclear programs. Key question: Is money actually coming?

Step 6

Proposal & Capture Execution

When something formal drops: lead capture strategy, write/coordinate proposals, manage win themes, cost story, executive reviews, compliance, red teams.

Step 7

Post-Award Transition

After a win: support contract negotiations, handoff to program managers, keep customer relationships warm, tee up follow-on work.

📜 Contract Vehicles Explained: The Complete Guide

Understanding the difference between contract types is critical. Each has different rules, timelines, and strategies.

⚡ OTA Other Transaction Authority

"Let's skip some red tape so we can prototype cutting-edge tech quickly."

A special contracting mechanism the U.S. government uses to move fast and work with non-traditional companies that might avoid normal federal contracts.

What Makes OTAs Different

  • Much faster: Weeks/months instead of years
  • More flexible: Negotiated terms, custom IP
  • Startup-friendly: Not FAR-compliant
  • Prototype-focused: Demos, test articles, pilots

Common OTA Use Cases

  • Demonstration systems
  • Pilot deployments
  • Early field tests
  • Risk-reduction programs
  • AI, autonomy, nuclear, space power

Who Uses OTAs

DoD labs, Space and missile agencies, Defense innovation offices (DIU), Combatant commands, Rapid-capabilities shops. Common in defense-tech, space power, AI, autonomy, and microreactors.

OTA Proposal Format

  • Technical solution narrative
  • Demo plan with objectives
  • Rough-order cost estimate
  • Schedule (12-24 months typical)
  • Company background & commercial relevance

📜 FAR Federal Acquisition Regulation (Traditional RFPs)

The formal system behind classic government procurements.

Used across DoD and NASA for major programs. Heavy compliance burden but stable funding for multi-year programs.

How FAR Works

  • Government publishes detailed RFP
  • Months to prepare proposals
  • Formal evaluation criteria
  • Audits, certifications, cost reviews
  • Protestable awards (losers can sue)

Typical Use Cases

  • Weapon systems
  • Satellites
  • Nuclear plants
  • Large IT systems
  • Production contracts

Pros

  • Stable multi-year funding
  • Big production contracts
  • Clear path to Programs of Record

Cons

  • Slow (12-36+ months)
  • Heavy compliance burden
  • Tough for startups
  • Expensive proposal process

🔬 BAA Broad Agency Announcement

R&D shops like DARPA use these for early-stage research and demonstrations.

BAA Characteristics

  • Rolling submissions
  • White papers first (5-8 pages)
  • If invited: full proposal (30-60 pages)
  • Competitive but startup-friendly
  • FAR-lite (less regulated)

What They Fund

  • Science & tech exploration
  • Early-stage research
  • Demonstrations
  • Phase 1/2/3 structured programs

Typical BAA Format

  • Technical area descriptions
  • Research goals & metrics
  • Expected funding ranges by phase
  • Evaluation criteria
  • Submission portal & deadlines

🔍 RFI Request for Information

Market research not contracts yet, but they shape future RFPs!

Agencies are testing the market: Who can do this? How mature is the tech? What approaches exist? What would it cost?

Typical RFI Contains

  • Background / mission need
  • Problem statement
  • Desired capabilities
  • Rough performance targets
  • Questions to industry
  • Instructions & due date

What You Submit (5-15 pages)

  • White-paper style PDF
  • High-level concept
  • Team description
  • Risks & mitigations
  • No pricing usually required

📝 Contract Formats: What You Actually Submit

Understanding the format determines your success. Each vehicle has different requirements.

RFI Response (5-15 pages)

Market research shapes future RFPs

  1. Executive Summary (1-2 paragraphs)
  2. Mission Applications
  3. Technical Approach
  4. Development Plan
  5. Safety & Launch Considerations
  6. Teaming Strategy
  7. Cost ROM
  8. Why Your Team

BAA White Paper (5-8 pages)

R&D funding invitation to full proposal

  1. Problem Statement
  2. Technical Approach
  3. Key Innovations
  4. Team (named individuals)
  5. Risks & Mitigations
  6. Schedule & Cost ROM

OTA Proposal (15-30 pages)

Fast prototyping without FAR compliance

  1. Technical Solution
  2. Prototype Plan
  3. Demo Objectives
  4. Schedule with Milestones
  5. Cost Breakdown
  6. IP/Data Rights
  7. Transition Path

Full FAR Proposal (60+ pages)

Traditional procurement heavy compliance

  1. Technical Volume (detailed approach)
  2. Management Volume (org, schedule)
  3. Past Performance Volume
  4. Cost Volume (detailed pricing)
  5. Compliance Matrix
  6. Certifications & Reps

Pro Tip: RFIs Shape RFPs

Smart companies treat RFI responses like mini proposals. Your input influences how the government frames requirements. Steer them toward what you can deliver.

🎯 Practical Tips: How New Entrants Actually Break In

Most successful companies do NOT start by trying to win a $500M prime contract. Here's the actual path:

1. Pick a Niche

  • Fuel fabrication
  • Thermal management
  • Shielding
  • Power conversion
  • Controls / autonomy
  • Heat pipes

2. Build Credibility

  • Win small SBIRs first
  • Partner with a national lab
  • Build non-nuclear prototypes
  • Publish technical papers
  • Get named nuclear engineers

3. Team Smart

  • Prime on small contracts
  • Sub to big primes on large ones
  • Sign MOUs early
  • Don't compete with your partners

4. Be Everywhere

  • Respond to every relevant RFI
  • Attend every Industry Day
  • Brief every PM who will listen
  • Get in working groups
