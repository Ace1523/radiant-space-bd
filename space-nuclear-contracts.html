🔔 How to Set Up Contract Alerts

  • 1. Register on SAM.gov

    Create account at sam.gov. Get your UEI and CAGE code. Required for all federal contracts.

  • 2. Set Up SAM.gov Saved Searches

    Keywords: "space nuclear", "fission surface power", "nuclear thermal", "reactor", "HALEU", "cislunar power". Enable email notifications.

  • 3. Register on SBIR.gov

    Create account at sbir.gov. Subscribe to topic areas: nuclear, space technology, power systems.

  • 4. Monitor DARPA Weekly

    No automated alerts - manually check DARPA opportunities every Monday.

  • 5. Follow NASA FSP Program

    Bookmark NASA Glenn FSP page. Industry days announced before SAM.gov postings.

    Defense SBIR/STTR Innovation Portal at dodsbirsttr.mil for DoD proposal submission.

♕ Contract Strategy & BD Playbook

📈 The 5-Phase BD Roadmap

How space nuclear programs progress from concept to production procurement.

0
Market Creation & Mission Shaping
Before programs exist. Create demand, define missions, influence requirements.

Activities: White papers, lunar-night analyses, industry conferences, prime briefings

Vehicles: Study contracts, SBIR Phase I, Concept BAAs

1
Concept & Risk Reduction
First technical money. Show space-adaptable designs work on paper and in labs.

Activities: Space-variant core designs, thermal-vacuum modeling, autonomy concepts

Vehicles: OTA prototypes, SBIR Phase II, BAAs

2
Integrated Ground Prototype
Full nuclear system on Earth. Run complete power system in test facilities.

Activities: Reactor hardware, control systems, safety packages, prime teaming

Vehicles: Large OTAs, Prototype BAAs ($5-25M)

3
Flight Demonstration Mission
First real deployment in space. Launch and operate reactor in orbit or on surface.

Activities: Flight core delivery, fuel coordination, nuclear safety inputs

Vehicles: Hybrid OTA to FAR demo ($20-100M+)

4
Operational Procurement
Infrastructure, not experiments. Buy fleets and sustain for decades. Multi-billion dollar programs.

Activities: Scale factories, secure HALEU supply, classified facilities, lifecycle ops

Vehicles: FAR-based production contracts, IDIQ vehicles

⚙ Technical Overview: Space Nuclear Reactors

🌍 Why Space Reactors Are Different

Space reactors must operate in brutally hostile conditions that Earth-based systems never face.

Environmental Challenges

  • Vacuum - No convective cooling possible
  • Microgravity - Affects fluid dynamics
  • Temperature swings - Lunar day/night: +260F to -280F
  • Radiation & micrometeoroids - Constant bombardment
  • Abrasive dust - Lunar regolith is sharp and clingy

Design Implications

  • Heat rejection relies on radiators only
  • Materials must survive massive thermal cycling
  • Moving parts minimized - passive systems dominate
  • Reliability beats efficiency in space
  • Mass is existential - every kg costs ~$5K-50K to orbit

🔥 How Fission Power Works

Fission is achieved by bombarding uranium atoms with neutrons. The uranium absorbs the neutron, becomes unstable, and splits apart - releasing energy plus more neutrons that continue the chain reaction.

Power Control

The amount of power is set by the number of neutrons in flight. Control this by:

  • Boron blades - Absorb neutrons when inserted (like Radiant's design)
  • Graphite reflectors - Bounce neutrons back into core
  • Slide boron in front of graphite to capture neutrons and reduce power

💫 Cooling & Power Conversion

Cooling Systems

  • Liquid-metal coolants - Sodium, NaK, lithium
  • Heat pipes - Embedded in core (passive)
  • Helium gas - Radiant's approach (can't become radioactive)

Power Conversion

  • Stirling engines - Good efficiency, moving parts
  • Brayton turbines - High power density
  • Thermoelectrics - Lower efficiency but ultra-reliable

🛡 Shielding Strategy

You can't encase a space reactor in concrete. Mass constraints demand creative solutions:

  • Directional shielding - Only shield between reactor and crew/electronics
  • Distance - Deploy on a boom or separate lander
  • Lightweight absorbers - Lithium hydride, borated polymers

☢ Fuel Technologies

HALEU

High-Assay Low-Enriched Uranium (5-20% U-235). Higher enrichment than commercial reactors but still below weapons-grade. Enables compact cores.

TRISO

Tri-structural isotropic particles. Tiny fuel kernels with multiple protective ceramic layers. Will not melt down - inherently safe even at extreme temperatures.

Zirconium Hydride

Radiant's innovation. Moderator material that lets you pack fission physics into shipping-container-scale systems. Enables highly compact core designs.

Helium Coolant

Cannot become radioactive in the reactor (unlike water or liquid metals). Simplifies safety case and reduces activation concerns.

🤖 Autonomy Requirements

Space reactors operate with:

  • No operators
  • No spare parts
  • No quick repairs

They must:

  • Self-start and self-regulate
  • Detect faults autonomously
  • Scram (emergency shutdown) without human intervention
  • Reconfigure loads automatically
  • Run for a decade+ untouched

Think: Nuclear plant + spacecraft flight computer in one box.

🚀 Launch & Deployment

Unique to space nuclear - political as much as technical:

  • Reactor must be sub-critical during launch
  • Survive vibration and abort scenarios
  • Only activate after safe deployment
  • Pass Presidential-level nuclear approval
  • Comply with UN treaties and international agreements
⚠

Launch approval is the long pole in the tent. Start this process early - it can take years.

📚 Historical Context: Space Nuclear Programs

🇺🇸 SNAP-10A (USA, 1965)

The only U.S. fission reactor flown in space.

Mission Details

  • Purpose: Prove fission-electric power in orbit
  • Power: ~600 watts electric
  • Duration: 43 days of operation
  • Status: Still in orbit today

Outcome

  • Started successfully - demonstrated the concept works
  • Shut down due to high-voltage failure in spacecraft (not reactor)
  • Intended to run ~1 year
  • Verdict: Technical success, duration failure

🇷🇺 Soviet/Russian Space Reactors (1967-1988)

The real "workhorse" history - over 30 fission reactors flown.

BES-5 "Buk" Reactors (RORSAT Program)

  • Purpose: Power ocean surveillance radar satellites
  • Why nuclear: Solar couldn't power radar, especially on night side
  • Count: 32 satellites launched with nuclear sources
  • Lifetimes: 44-131 days operational

TOPAZ Thermionic Reactors

  • Higher efficiency than thermoelectric systems
  • Kosmos-1818: ~6 months operation
  • Kosmos-1867: ~1 year operation
⚠

The Kosmos 954 Incident (1978)

Soviet RORSAT satellite lost control, failed to boost to disposal orbit. Reentered over Canada, spreading radioactive debris across ~124,000 km2. Canada billed USSR $6 million for cleanup. Drove international liability framework for space nuclear.

💥 Key Problems From Historical Programs

Technical Challenges

  • Power conversion: Getting useful electricity from reactor heat
  • Heat rejection: Dumping waste heat via radiators in vacuum
  • Reliability: Single failure = mission loss (SNAP-10A)
  • Coolant leakage: TOPAZ missions had post-mission issues

Political/Operational

  • End-of-life disposal: Failed boost to burial orbit = disaster
  • International blowback: Reentry events create liability
  • Public fear: "Nuclear in space" is politically sensitive
  • Regulatory burden: Years of approval process

🌎 International Competition: Russia & China

This is a race. They are going to do it faster if we don't act.

🇷🇺 Russia

  • Publicly discussed lunar nuclear power station
  • Target timeline: ~2036
  • Tied to lunar base roadmap
  • Partnership with China on ILRS

🇨🇳 China

  • Target: mid-2030s for lunar surface
  • Megawatt-class space concepts discussed publicly
  • High-power nuclear for space propulsion research
